# Home / Ceremony
Possible reading for Mimosa and Geoff  
 
Paul Woodruff
 
Marriage is a commitment to making a home together.  A shelter, a roof over your head, is not a home. Countless small acts of loving ceremony make a shelter into a home, ceremony at mealtime, at day’s end or beginning, at games or entertainments, often shared silently among those for whom the place is home. You do not make a home by yourself. Even if you live alone, and *are* at home, you are linked to ancestors or to absent children by things you do or by relics that you honor. Home holds reminders of the rise and fall of families—a picture, an old toy, a great-grandmother’s table—but whatever it contains a home is the center for a web of love and respect across generations. The ceremony that makes a home is an expression of reverence; ceremony that does this cannot be an empty routine, and, for that reason, it cannot be rigid and unchanging. Every day you must do some loving thing to make sure that whatever roof your share is your true home.  
 
*Modified from Reverence, Renewing a Forgotten Virtue (2d ed., 2014)*
