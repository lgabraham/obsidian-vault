# Dear Mom

Mom,

I am happy you came to the Broadmoor. I  enjoyed seeing you open to folks who love you and only want good things for you. I was happy to see them open to you, too. 

Yesterday morning, your energy struck a different tone. I’d guess the cacophony of togetherness wore on you. I’d guess it was difficult to be with this group with the obvious absence of your husband. 

I’d guess that while the group opened their hearts to find joy in togetherness, when you opened your heart you still found grief and pain. Anger, too. 

I do believe the group could have held space for this experience. I know that I am capable of doing so. I also understand why it might have felt like too much for you to reveal. 

Rather than open your heart for others to hold, you withdrew. You remained withdrawn in spite of several well-intentioned bids for your return. At least to me, it seemed you also wished to make your withdrawal known, at least to me. 

Withdrawing into pain, as in Soul, warrants empathy. As in Soul, sometimes only you can reach you. 

All of this hurt me. I imagine it was not your first intent. Yet it hurt me still. Among other things, I missed hearing warm words from you when saying goodbyes.

Before coming to the Broadmoor, you expressed concern that I be protected from certain family dynamics. I know, therefore, you want me to be protected from pain. 

You may not know, or you may not be able to see because of your own pain, that your behavior affected me. Thus I am writing you to let you know. 

I’m also writing because I believe you wish to live in courage, even when it is hard. This is the stuff of Maria Shriver, Brene Brown, Oprah, etc. It means taking ownership of your emotions, even when they are hard.

I feel more confident writing you on the precipice of parenthood. I hope my child is born into love, and only love. I would want to know if a behavior of mine hurt them, especially if I was not aware. 

I would have preferred to say this in person, as all emotional communication happens best in person. This would have been my courage.

I hope that you find rest in your final day. I hope you find appropriate space for your feelings, especially when they are hard. 

Love,
Geoff
—-

It is a kind of cry for help. This is how I understood your behavior. 

This is not the first of this pattern. It is the stuff of my therapy. It could be the stuff of yours. 

I know ultimately this pattern of behavior is not about me. Even though it is sometimes hard for me to understand. 

Much like a parent, I am trying to find a way to love you, and not tolerate certain behavior. 

Our lives will only grow more complex in the next months. My capacity to care-take your emotions — “be your interpreter” — will decrease as I grow into my own life. 

And to be honest, being your interpreter takes a toll on me. 

I have spent many months making sure you are taken care of in your experience. Now I wish to take care of myself in mine.
