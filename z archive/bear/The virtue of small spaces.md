# The virtue of small spaces

Recently, I stayed at one of the houses of one of the wealthiest people in the world. 

Picture of entrance. 

He wasn’t using it. He had eaten there — hosted dinners with important people — but not stayed there in some time. It would make for an impressive dinner. 

The first thing that struck me was the expanse of it. It was 16,000 square feet.  The average American house is, the average metropolitan house is. So it was not a house but a villa — a villa, on a many acre property. 

Its best feature was its relationship with nature. We woke up to thick Bay fog. We overlooked Silicon Valley.  

It was an old house. I spent a lot of time looking for light switches, and outlets. The showers had been elaborately renovated yet had no water pressure. 

My friend was pleasantly surprised that this person’s fabulously wealthy life was not so dissimilar from our own.

And indeed for all of the house that this was, it did not feel like a home. 

Labrynthine. I didn’t know where to sit. I literally sat in the floor bc I couldn’t find comfort There were chandeliers in the bedroom

There were pictures of his family, but I know his family is broken. Indeed, the house had a history of broken families. 

The family that built the house had one daughter. She fell in love with a gas station attendant. Her parents pressured her to divorce him. Then she committed suicide on the property. 

When the house was purchased by another wealthy person, his heirs fought over it. 

Even the description pointed out the burden of owning “such large estates”. “There is a subset of wealthy individuals and families willing to take on the management of a large estate but it can become a full time job and the subset isn’t a big fraction of the home owning public”

For most of this houses life, more people worked there than lived in it. 

What a counter intuitive thought. This house was a poor home. Making a home is hard. 

I used to live in a former horse barn in San Francisco. The family that had built the house in front of it made carriages, and the barn is where they kept the horses. 500 sq ft. 2 rooms. 

My friends, who would stay there when I was traveling, would often comment on how rejuvenated they felt when they left. The home was “cozy” “tucked away” and had a “lived in” magic to it. Some joked about it being a kind of hobbit home. 

I lived a happy life there. It expanded to fit my girlfriend, now wife. We hosted parties of 30-50 people. We hosted Friendsgivings. 

We made it work. 

Screwtape letters. CS Lewis describes hell as a constantly expanding suburb, where when people have an argument, they simply move to their own home. 

It’s evocative, and I was reminded of the wealthy persons many houses, and the houses with many rooms. 

It also points out the dangers of wealth. There are 10 divorces in the 7 wealthiest people in the world. When you stop having to make it work, it often doesn’t work. 

Wealth complicates the question. But I have the hunch that if wealthy people had to stay in small spaces with their families, they’d be happier.  The problem with wealth is that it can isolate you, even from people you love. Example. And be less out of touch. 

The virtues of the small space is having to make it work. 

If only bc big spaces are unrepeatable, they isolate you. 
At the start of this project, I started thinking of a home by its verbs rather than its space. A place to sleep, eat, work. 

I know the villa did some things well. Hosted dinners. 

A well-functioning home must do many things well. 

What things? The subject of another post. 

I laughed seeing a squirrel on a rail. Would the squirrel know this was an “important” home? No. It was empty. 

If I could see so many homes from 10,000 feet, which would be the most desirable? The shape of a loving home is a person or small group of people that are constantly there, and larger groups of people that come, and the constant group occasionally goes away. 

The house I was in was less a house and more of a museum. 

The idea of feeling contained.
