# The activities of a home

I’ve been speaking to my friends about what they do in their home. 

Home means many different things to different people. The meaning is informed by so many things that are deeply personal. 

Yet what we do in the home, whether or not you live in a large home or a small home (link), largely looks the same. 

Activities of a home 

Sleep - Whatever we do at our home, many of us will sleep there more than anything else. sleep has become in vogue. 

We might expand sleep to include rest — lounging in pajamas on the couch is a precursor or post cursor to sleep. 

Eat - When people are awake, at least at most homes I’ve visited, everyone seems to end up in the kitchen. 

Eating is the prime industry of the home. What I mean by this is that sleep is passive and so requires little work. Yes, it may require routines. 

But to eat, you must prepare food, and all that this entails. You must have money to pay for the food, you must acquire it, you must know or learn how to cook it, and you must serve it. All before eating it. 

This is a lot of work. This is why often families lean into division of labor (one primary meal preparer). One exception to this I know of is uniform meals that either partner prepares. The point is that families have to figure out, among themselves, who is doing this work. 

Indeed in most of the world, most meals are home-made, ie they are made by families eating for themselves or with others. And thank goodness, because eating this way nourishes the soul in a way eating takeout or at a fancy restaurant does not. 

In my family, my wife prepares the meals. Her father had been the meal preparer in her family. He had a garden that provided much of her family’s food. His joy for growing and preparing food rubbed off on her.

This is a special case of convening, ie communing — bonding over a shared meal. This is my favorite family time. We talk about our days, a trip we might go on, what’s been hard for us. 

Work - We have to pay for the food. Not just the food but the house itself, and the utilities that make the house livable. 

After Covid, many homes became sometime or partial offices. 

Bathe - Humans like to be clean. And at least most other people in our lives prefer that we’re clean. The natural place to clean oneself, consistently, is the home. 

I might expand bathing to include other rituals of self care — sauna, cold plunge, etc. 

Be together - I love to have people over. And I love to go over to other people’s home. It is such an intimate act. 

Be alone - Even if you live with a large chaotic family, you likely have one place, that some of the time, you can be alone, and this space is likely special to you. 

Of course, you might be alone bathing, or working, or getting ready for bed. 

You also might spend most of your time alone in your home, whether you wish to or not. 

Leave/return - My 2yo daughter likes to say goodbye to our home when we leave it. It’s an emotionally pregnant moment. Leaving the safety of “our” space out into the world. We like to say we are going on a family adventure. 

Similarly, we like to greet our home when we return. Again, a pregnant moment because you are returning to safety, like a bird returns to its nest. The bird rests from flying, hunting. If no dangers are near, the bird can now safely rest. 

It’s also important to not that these are the functions of life itself. Even if you don’t have a physical home, you still do these activities *somewhere*. 

I left out activities you likely do. Watching movies with family is being together. Playing video games your room migh be being alone or together (digitally connected). 

Yet I think these are the most important activities that happen at the home. 

It follows that a well-functioning home supports these activities, and further follows that it supports these activities for *all* of the house’s inhabitants. 

This is a lot of activity. Even for a house of 3 like my own, we must all eat several times a day, all find time to work (my 2 year old’s work is play), shower, find time and space to be together and to be alone. 


While it might seem overwhelming to consider trying to have a small space do all of these activities, there is a lucky phenomenon, which is that there is great virtue in trying to make all of these things work in a small space. 

It might be good then that we are trying to compress these activities into smaller and smaller spaces. 

One of the funny things we’ve found while doing this project is that there are a lot of “standard” terms that don’t make sense. Rather than having a “living room” and a “dining room”, I might have a living room that is also a dining room. 

The verbs of a home help clarify for me what is important. Where do I eat alone? Where do I eat when we are together? Is the place where sleep my “bedroom” set up for sleep? How do I feel when I return home?

These questions help me better understand what I want in a home, before I get overwhelmed by all the other things I must consider when making a home. 

This can also clarify which furniture you actually want versus what other people tell you you need.
