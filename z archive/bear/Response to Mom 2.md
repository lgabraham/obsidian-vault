# Response to Mom


Mom,

You absolutely did take an emotional risk in coming. It was beautiful to behold. May you always. I hope you dance.

Being alone and feeling alone are not the same thing. In this case, you describe feeling alone while being around others who love you.

I do not remember retiring Saturday night without saying goodnight. Your experience is valid. I won’t argue the point. 

Interpreting this event is a choice. “I’m sure they’re overwhelmed” is a choice of trust. “They abandoned me” is a choice that will leave you feeling alone.

Of course I understand why you might feel alone. Feeling alone is natural, especially in your current moment in life. Choosing what to do about those feelings is another matter. And in a way, I am asking you to make different choices.

If you tell me, “I feel alone”, you have made a choice towards our relationship. You can tell me why, and I can listen. Perhaps I can comfort you so that you don’t feel so alone. 

If you instead hope that I read your mind, and then withdrawal when I do not, it only leaves you feeling more alone. You have made a choice away from our relationship, a choice that can snowball on itself.

Mimosa and I do appreciate the space you give us. The integrity of our relationship is important. Yet we also rather have you ask for what you want than feel alone. 

The weekend was itself emotionally complex for me, and I felt pulled in many directions.  My attention on or not on you was not entirely about you. 

I will admit that when you begin to withdrawal — and of course my inner child is always hyper aware of this — it makes me not want to further engage with you. I do not wish to emotionally “chase” you. This feels unhealthy.

We did, in fact, intend to invite you for a Sunday coffee. We then got swept up in other togetherness. This is sometimes how chaotic social gatherings go.

I too savor our togetherness. I understand craving this. I understand, too, wondering about when is appropriate to invite versus be invited. I struggle with this too.

I do also enjoy seeing these other lovely, fun Abraham + folks. I do hope my inner-circles of family can integrate into larger groups as well as have our own sacred togetherness. This capacity speaks to the security of our foundation.

In general, I invite you to lean into asking, and to choose trust in the face of both “no” and “yes” alike. 

Please help me co-create our togetherness. Our togetherness is a collective act. You are a participant. It is a burden on me to be its only architect. Expecting me to “read your mind” sets us up for failure. It is also not fair to me, your child.

What if you invited us to a coffee or night cap? You can trust us to tell you “no” if this is not something we want. I know this “leap” can feel scary. Asking for what you want leaves you exposed. This is vulnerability; it is courage.

At its heart, I believe the above pattern of behavior is ultimately about your relationship with yourself — about whether you feel worthy, whether you feel self-love, whether you can feel confident you are loved by others.

The security you and Dad impart to me I will never take for granted. It is one of the greatest gifts I know. It’s not just the brick and mortar. It is the how-to of how to build a home.

Yet what I really want from you is not merely the bones of a happy home life but you involved in it. I want your love, your support, and your presence. I want you to be a loving and engaged mother to an adult son.

This cannot be purchased.  Nor can it be given once. It is actively given each day. 

I feel confident asking this of you because I am a loving, engaged adult son. I am not asking you to be perfect. I am asking you to listen to the effect your behavior has on me, and to listen to what I am asking of you.

I do believe you did the best you knew how. The strange magic of emotions is that their intensity expands the aperture of life. Claiming my own experience — being scared or angry about stress I experienced — also enables me to feel deeper gratitude and love for the blessings I have. Higher highs, and lower lows. I cannot “selectively mute” my emotions.

To be sure, my child will attend therapy. In fact, I enjoy this definition of successful parenting: your child is in therapy for lesser things than you yourself are in therapy for.

I am not currently interested in prosecuting the past. You were and are a wonderful Mom. 

I am interested in creating a sane present and future mother-son relationship, so that it may sustain and flourish. 

Love,
Geoff

It is true that part of this may require work from you, especially if you find yourself often feeling alone among those that love you.
