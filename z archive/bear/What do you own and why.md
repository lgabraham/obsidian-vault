# What do you own and why?
You are what you own.

**Savings, a Composition of You.**
I love saving. When I save a dollar, it becomes “capital”. When I invest that dollar, it becomes a bet. 
Buying a house is a bet. Buying a stock is a bet. Selling is a bet I can better use with the proceeds. Holding cash in a bank is an option to make bets in the future.
Of course, a bet often isn’t *strictly* financial. I hope my home will appreciate in value; it is not the only reason I purchased a home. Bets are often *importantly/financial. I likely wouldn’t buy a home if I thought it would /decrease* in value. The core belief behind a financial bet is that the bet will “pay off” — it will be worth more in the future than it is today. 
I may be more or less conscious and conscientious of the decision to make a bet and my intention as to why I make a bet. I may save in a 401K without knowing what this means. I may have inherited some money or property. I may have bought a car on impulse. 

Alternatively, I may have carefully considered why I believe in the bet and what it means to me. I may have studied the market to buy a valuable home. I may have spent a long time deciding why I wanted to live in a particular place or own a particular stock.

Life is chaotic enough that likely your savings is not perfectly ordered. At any given moment, I may ask myself: Do I believe in the bets I am making? Do I know what they mean to me?

This question becomes far more complicated as I make multiple bets. I may own a home and a 401K *and* an impulse car at the same time I may try to save for my child’s college fund. Now I must consider what each of these bets mean *in context to one another*.

This leads to a question even more interesting than the questions about individual bets. Now I may ask: What does the composition of my savings say about me?

I want to pause here to acknowledge that this is a question of privilege. Even having the mental space to consider this question — in between working, parenting, living, etc. — is a privilege. And yet answering this question seems important regardless of how much wealth I have.

Asking this question offers an opportunity to become more aware of how my financial life is organized. It allows me the chance to be more intentional about this organization.

This matters all matters for two reasons. 

First, how I decide to save is a source of tremendous leverage in my financial life. This is primarily true because of the dynamics of compounding. If the total rate of return of my overall savings is even one or two percentage points higher, I may be multiples better off later in life.

Second, more importantly, I naturally hope to make a life of meaning. Taking greater ownership over my total stock of savings — from a house down to the computer I’m typing on — allows me to make greater meaning of the things that I own. 

I implicitly participate in capital markets, sure. But can I *intentionally* participate in capital markets? I implicitly believe the world will be better off if we each do this. I also naturally believe that each of us wish to do this.

Ok, capitalist hippie. It’s all well and good to make intentional bets in free markets. What does this mean, exactly?

The relative size of each of my bets may be a good heuristic for its importance to me. Is this reflected in the composition of my savings? If not, how might I change this?

Is my stock of savings concentrated in one kind of thing — say, all in Bitcoin, or all in equity in the company I am building? Concentration is a high-risk, high-reward strategy. It is how wealth is built and lost.  Is my stock of savings diversified among many sets of things? This is often a more conservative and defensive strategy. 

More important than the answer to either question, am I aware of this strategy and what it means to me? Do I committed to this strategy at this moment in my life?

This all became important to me because my father did not seem to think deeply about these questions. He was a successful entrepreneur. But once he had made a decent pile of savings, he then went buying and selling things according to his whim. 

It’s not that his whimsical saving and spending didn’t work for him. He died of a heart attack before he could spend all of his money. In some ways, this justified the pace of his aggressive spending. Yet I learned by watching him that I hoped to be more intentional with how I save.

Taken in sum, capital markets are betting mechanisms. We all implicitly participate in markets any time we save. What does the composition of your savings say about you?
