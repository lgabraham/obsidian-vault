2. Our company provides a fast way to discover furniture online.

3. Buying physical things on the internet is hard. You must quality-check something without touching it, double-check dimensions for where the thing will go, and evaluate a sellers’s credibility often with little data. Buying furniture online is a special case of hard. It is *especially* hard. And it is harder for three reasons. 

First, in no one location can you quickly and easily search all the furniture that is sold. This is true, even though the number of furniture makers and the items they make is relatively small.

Next, when you can search furniture in a limited way, it is difficult to compare like with like. Sellers often rename the same exact item or produce different images to the same item. Both of these tactics fool traditional search engines. 

Finally, many search experiences aren’t built with the understanding that you might not know what you want, or how to begin to understand this. What is the difference between a credenza and a buffet, anyway? This more complex because buying furniture can be deeply emotional. It can come to define your home.

This matters because we are buying furniture online more than ever before. This won’t reverse, and it shouldn’t. Browsing across the entire inventory of the internet should be more compelling than going to a physical store. Moreover, you *should* be able to sanely, confidently buy furniture online. 

4. We arrived at this problem by encountering it ourselves. Last year, I (Dane) moved across the country and went through the back-breaking ordeal of furnishing a new apartment. After purchasing and assembling several items, I uncovered that all of the furniture I had just set up could have been found elsewhere for cheaper — like 20-50% cheaper.

My predominant feeling was anger. I soon found out by speaking to others I was not alone in this feeling. I won’t lie: we enjoy that our natural position is one of a People’s Champion. Users have consistently told us we are “doing the lord’s work.”

5. The first problem — no one location exists for search — is primarily one of incentives. Tech giants simply won’t include other tech giants on their own platforms. 

Some shopping comparison extensions and applications might show you some places you can buy items more cheaply, but they are also in cahoots with their partners. They won’t show you everywhere you can.

We intend to show you everywhere you can buy the same thing, which we believe unlocks a lot of consumer value.

The second problem — that it is difficult to compare like with like — is an interesting technical problem, especially at scale.

The third problem I mention — that searching for furniture is a matter of preference discovery and may be deeply emotional — is a problem of empathy and design. This less technical. We simply want to make discovering furniture as enjoyable as discovering your next AirBnB.

Another thing that separates us is the way we engage with our community. Users submit furniture “dupes” for us to add to our inventory. There is such power in this. Once one person has “done the work” of connecting an item, we should all reap the benefit of this. We are designing tools to further empower our users.

Each of the technical problems is challenging on their own, but we also believe there is something special about solving them together.

You can go to our site to peruse furniture. You can save items to lists. We have also built a chrome extension that allows you to browse for furniture as you normally would. We simply alert you if the item you’re seeing can be purchased elsewhere — at how many stores and for what price difference.
