# Excerpts from "A Grief Observed" by C.S. Lewis:
Already, less than a month after her death, I can feel the slow, insidious beginning of a process that will make the H. I think of into a more and more imaginary woman... The reality is no longer there to check me, to pull me up short, as the real H. so often did, so unexpectedly, by being so thoroughly herself and not me... The most precious gift that marriage gave me was this constant impact of something very close to intimate yet all the time unmistakably other, resistant -- in a word, real.

The starboard engine is gone. I, the port engine, must chug along until we make harbor.

And suddenly at the very moment when, so far, I mourned H. least, I remembered her best. Indeed it was something (almost) better than memory: an instantaneous, unanswerable impression... After all, you must have the capacity to receive, or even omnipotence can't give.

"How often -- will it be for always? -- how often will the vast emptiness astonish me like a complete novelty and make me say, 'I never realized my loss until this moment'? The same leg is cut off time after time. The first plunge of the knife into the flesh is felt again and again."

"All reality is iconoclastic. The earthly beloved, even in this life, incessantly triumphs over your mere idea of her. And you want her to; you want her with all her resistances, all her faults, all her unexpectedness. That is, in her foursquare and independent reality. And this, not any image or memory, is what we are to love still, after she is dead."
