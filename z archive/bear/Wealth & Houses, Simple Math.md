# Wealth & Houses, Simple Math

ENTERING THE RACE 

I recently bought a home in Oakland. In the lead up to purchasing this home, I witnessed what seemed like a kind of insanity — 20 offers per home, purchase prices 50% above listing prices, and homes lasting less than 2 days on the market.

We happened to be purchasing a home at a particularly stressful moment  for buyers in the Bay. COVID19 had depressed demand in the Spring, and pent-up demand had resurged in Fall. 

Moreover, many City of SF residents like myself, recognizing that the virus would last for many more months, were looking for more space precisely in the areas my wife and I were looking.

Yet the chronic structural imbalance between available houses and available wealth had long been on my mind. Now, with direct experience of upward price pressure, I began to wonder about the simple math behind seemingly relentless upward pressure of home prices in my beloved Bay Area. 

THE SIMPLE MATH OF INCREASING HOME PRICES

The City of San Francisco has roughly 400,000 homes and 800,000 humans. The wider metropolitan area has  roughly 2.5M homes and 5M humans. [SOURCE] 

I haven’t discovered exactly how much wealth is concentrated in the Bay Area. I do know roughly $6.3T of wealth exists in California. [SOURCE] If we assume that 20% of this wealth resides in the Bay Area, which seems conservative, then $1.3T of wealth resides in the Bay Area. This equates to about $500,000 of wealth per available home.

In addition to existing wealth, the Bay Area generates about $500B in economic output each year. (This is growing at a pace of +$20B each year.)  [SOURCE]  This resolves to about $200,000 of income per available home. With some perhaps overly simple assumptions (33% of income towards mortgage and property insurance, 2.5% mortgage interest), this equates to an additional $2M of “purchasing power” per available home. [SOURCE] 

[ Footnote ] This may not include “invested capital” in startups that are not generating income. $200B lost on Quibli. 

To summarize, there is roughly $2.5M of available purchasing power for every home in the Bay Area. The median price of a Bay Area home is around $1M. [SOURCE] 

The most obviously grotesque component of these assumptions is that the wealth and income are evenly distributed. They are not. In fact, they are wildly skewed toward the most privileged.

Another obvious oversimplification is that not all fo this wealth or income will be put towards a home. Though I would contend, pointing to Maslow’s hierarchy, that houses are among the first things that this wealth will go towards.

 More to the point of this piece of writing, the above wealth and income are not chasing *every* home. They are chasing *available* homes. 

One of the most remarkable things about San Francisco is its ability to produce *new* wealth and income. The roughly $6T of aforementioned purchasing power will be largely skewed toward purchasing new homes.

At any given month, roughly 1,000 homes will become available to be purchase in San Francisco. Roughly 8,000 homes will become available for purchase in the wider Bay Area. Put another way, about 100,000 homes will become available every year in the Bay. [SOURCE] 

If we imagined that even 10% of the aforementioned purchasing power is new and therefore new homes, then we would guess that some $600B of purchasing power is chasing 100,000 homes. This resolves to $6M per available home. Again, the median home price in the Bay is $1M.

[ Footnote ] This does not even take into account the pressures of non-Bay generational wealth. Millennials own ~3% of U.S. wealth. At a similar time in their lives, Boomers owned 35%. The result is that many Boomers, now sitting on wealth they cannot consume in the remainder of their lives, are looking to help their children buy homes. My mother helped me get a loan for our home. She does not live in the Bay. Anecdotally, I know of several other similar situations. 

Because securing a home seems so central to wellbeing to the American psyche, whole families may throw the kitchen sink at procuring one. Mine did.

But then this may be a wash because of wealth flowing the other way — Bay area families helping their children buy homes in other areas.

And thus 20 offers, +50% list price, etc. Despite my recent experience purchasing a home, my honest-to-God assessment is that homes could be 2x more expensive, and still receive bids. Securing a home is central to wellbeing.

None of the above discussion refers to interests rates, immigration to and from San Francisco, housing policy history, etc. Even before taking into account any of these things, a central economic reality of the Bay Area is that the average economic wealth and output of the area far exceeds the median current prices for available inventory of homes. Until this reverses, home prices will continue to climb. 

PERSISTENT SUPPLY CONSTRAINTS & DEMAND MOMENTUM

Personally, I believe the only possible viable solution to the above “problem” is increased supply of housing. This isn’t a belief based on policy, per se. It is a belief rooted in a respect of math, and an belief that the increased economic is both desirable and persistent.

Why is the housing inventory so low? In a traditional model of supply and demand, price will force an equilibrium between supply and demand. Why hasn’t inventory responded as such?

For one thing, local government has proven incapable of coordinating building new houses at scale. They may be getting worse, not better, at devising such solutions. 

In addition to this, there are two dynamics that exacerbate the chronic structural imbalance between supply and demand — rent control and Proposition 13.

Roughly 65% of San Franciscans, and 45% of Bay Area folks rent their homes. Nearly 40% of San Franciscan’s enjoy rent control, which caps their rent at 2% a year. 

My neighbor enjoys rent control. He sublets. While he does not make a profit, which would be illegal, he has pushed the majority of the rent burden onto newcomers to the City. Last month, he bought a home in Vallejo and will keep his apartment in the City because it is so cheap.

Meanwhile, all owners, the 65% of homes owned by landlords and 35% of home owners homeowners, enjoy the effects of Proposition 13, which cap annual property increases at 2% per year. 

As just one data point, my in law’s home is worth more than the home I just purchased. They pay $4,500 per year in property taxes. I will pay $20,000. 

No matter what you believe about these policies, the clear outcome of both is a disincentive to leave a cost-controlled home, thus removing the home from available inventory. When you add the additional incentive of inevitable further house price appreciation, which will either make your home more valuable or your controlled rent more attractive, the disincentive seems even stronger.

WINNERS & LOSERS

The result of all of this is an increased burden and gradient of achieving on the newcomers to the City. More pointedly, the newcomers who are not attached to the tech economy that is generating the lion’s share of the new wealth. “Good jobs” outside of the Bay  — nurses, optometrists, etc. — are not good jobs in the Bay. My average peer in tech out-earns their non-tech peers by 2x-3x.

In the last month alone, acquisitions (Twilio acquires Segment, Snowflake IPOs) have generated thousands of liquid cash millionaires who still have $250K+ salaries. 

If you are not central to the tech wealth engine, not supported by generational wealth, or not scrappy as hell, your prospects of purchasing a home in our beloved Bay seem very low.

At the tail end of all this housing pressure, of course, is homelessness or “the unhoused”. No matter what you believe are the root causes of homelessness, the above economic reality is adding pressure. At the current rate, this problem will worsen indefinitely.

THE FUTURE OF THE BAY

Some believe that San Francisco will collapse in the wake of a new wave of remote work. I don’t buy it. There is too much gravity and earnest inspiration in the machine that generates wealth.  Remote work, at least for the core team, is too dull, and humans yearn to be together. (Google just purchased new office space in the City.) 

Sure, there is no reason why other Cities can’t begin to generate wealth and the same way San Francisco does. I hope they do. There are plenty of disenchanted San Franciscans to contribute to this cause.

As for me, the Bay is still the home of my primary community, my wife’s parents, my most inspirational and inspired moments, and as much beauty as my heart can hold. I am privileged to have secured a home.

In the meantime, the chronic structural imbalance will persist, perhaps indefinitely — and near certainly for the next 20 years.
