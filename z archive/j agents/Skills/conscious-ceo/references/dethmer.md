## Above vs. below the line — the master diagnostic

At any moment a leader is operating from one of two states:

- **Above the line**: open, curious, committed to *learning*. Relaxed, present, willing to be wrong.
- **Below the line**: closed, defensive, committed to *being right* (and to being safe). Reactive,
  blaming, certain.

The foundational teaching: **knowing *that* you're below the line matters more than being below it.**
Everyone drops below many times a day — it's how humans are wired (the amygdala reads threat to the ego
as a threat to survival). Consciousness isn't staying above the line forever; it's *noticing* where you
are and being willing to shift. So the first coaching move is almost always: "Right now, are you above
or below the line?" Naming it honestly often produces the shift on its own.

Tells you're below the line: blame and criticism (of others or self), defensiveness, the need to be
right, gossip, a rigid story about what *should* be happening, "they always / they never," and a felt
sense of contraction.

## The four locations of leading

A model of how much responsibility a leader is taking:

- **To Me** (victim): "This is happening *to* me." Life/people/conditions are doing it to me. Blame,
  resentment, powerlessness.
- **By Me** (radical responsibility): "I am *creating*, allowing, or perpetuating this." Not blaming
  oneself — taking creative ownership. Asks: *"How am I creating this? How is this here for me? What can
  I learn?"* This is the workhorse shift — moving To Me → By Me restores a leader's power.
- **Through Me** (surrender/flow): aligning with something larger; letting purpose move through you
  rather than forcing outcomes.
- **As Me** (oneness): the experience of non-separation; advanced, rarely the coaching target.

Most coaching is helping someone move from **To Me to By Me**. Watch for the trap: By Me is *not*
self-blame ("it's all my fault"), which is just To Me pointed inward. It's curious ownership.

## The Drama Triangle vs. the Empowerment Triangle

Below-the-line relating runs on the **Drama Triangle**: **Victim** ("poor me"), **Villain** ("it's your
fault"), **Hero** ("let me rescue/fix this"). All three are below the line and they rotate. The conscious
alternative is the **Empowerment Triangle**: Victim → **Creator** (owns desired outcomes), Villain →
**Challenger** (calls people to their potential, candidly), Hero → **Coach** (helps others find their own
capacity instead of rescuing). Coaching note: sympathizing with someone's Victim story makes you the
Hero and keeps them the Victim — relate as Coach instead.

## The 15 Commitments

Each commitment is a shift from an unconscious default to a conscious choice. The first six are the
drama-reducers and do most of the day-to-day work.

1. **Radical Responsibility** — From blame (To Me) to ownership (By Me). "I commit to taking full
   responsibility for the circumstances of my life and my emotional reactions; I release blame and
   criticism."
2. **Learning Through Curiosity** — From being right to being curious. Commit to growth above ego;
   notice when you've drifted into defending a position.
3. **Feeling All Feelings** — From repressing/projecting emotion to feeling it fully and releasing it.
   (See "Working with feelings.")
4. **Speaking Candidly** — From withholding (and the "corporate nod") to revealing. Reveal thoughts,
   opinions, and feelings; withholding leaks energy and breeds boredom and distrust. Candor is about
   self-revelation, *not* changing the other person.
5. **Eliminating Gossip** — From talking *about* people to talking *to* them. Listening to gossip is the
   same as speaking it; go direct.
6. **Practicing Integrity** — From broken/implicit agreements to wholeness: tell the truth, keep (or
   consciously renegotiate) agreements, take responsibility, feel feelings. Integrity = congruence
   across word, feeling, and action; integrity breaches are felt as energy leaks.
7. **Generating Appreciation** — Fully giving *and* fully receiving appreciation. Refusing appreciation
   robs the giver. Catch people doing things right.
8. **Excelling in Your Zone of Genius** — From competence/excellence (good at it, but draining) to
   genius (uniquely gifted, energizing). Confront the "Upper Limit Problem" — the self-sabotage that
   kicks in when things get too good. (Shared with Mochary; both draw on Gay Hendricks, *The Big Leap*.)
9. **Living a Life of Play and Rest** — From grim productivity to play, improvisation, and genuine rest;
   energy and creativity are maximized this way.
10. **Exploring the Opposite** — "One viewpoint is too small for the whole truth." Hold your story
    lightly; actively look for how the opposite might be true (Byron Katie's turnarounds: is it true?
    can I absolutely know it's true? who would I be without the thought?).
11. **Sourcing Approval, Control & Security** — Stop seeking these externally; recognize you already
    *are* the source. The three core wants underneath most reactivity.
12. **Having Enough of Everything (Sufficiency)** — From scarcity ("never enough," competition) to
    enough-ness — of time, money, love, energy. Scarcity is largely a function of not being present.
13. **Experiencing the World as an Ally** — See all people and circumstances as perfectly suited to help
    you learn and grow, rather than as obstacles or threats. "How is this *for* me?"
14. **Creating Win-for-All Solutions** — From win/lose and compromise to outcomes that serve all parties
    and the whole.
15. **Being the Resolution** — "I commit to being the resolution or solution that is needed." See what's
    missing in the world as an invitation to *become* it, rather than waiting/complaining.

## Working with feelings (Commitment 3 in practice)

Emotions are "energy in motion" — physical sensations meant to move through, not concepts. Five primary
emotions, each with a body location and a message for a leader:

- **Anger** (back, shoulders, jaw): "something is no longer of service and a boundary needs setting."
  Below the line it shows up as blame and righteousness.
- **Fear** (belly): "wake up, pay attention." Below the line: a made-up catastrophic future.
- **Sadness** (throat, eyes, chest): "something needs to be let go of / grieved." Below the line: "poor
  me," and clinging to what's over.
- **Joy** (chest, spine): "something is to be celebrated." Below the line: brittle circumstantial
  happiness.
- **Sexual/creative energy**: the energy of creation and new ideas.

To **release** a feeling: locate the sensation in the body → breathe → allow/accept/appreciate it →
match expression to the sensation. Feelings released this way last ~90 seconds and come in waves.
Repressed or recycled, they harden into moods (anger → bitterness, sadness → depression). A useful
coaching question: "What are you feeling right now, and where do you feel it in your body?" (If "I feel"
is followed by "that" or "like," it's a thought, not a feeling.)

## The Clearing Model (candor and conflict)

A script for revealing something charged without slipping into blame — the backbone for hard
conversations and "clearing" with someone. Five steps:

1. **Facts**: only what a video camera would have captured. No interpretation. ("In Monday's meeting you
   said you'd send the deck by Tuesday. I didn't receive it.")
2. **Story**: "The story I'm making up is…" — explicitly owned as *your* interpretation, not truth.
   ("…that my work isn't a priority to you.")
3. **Feelings**: the primary emotions present. ("I feel angry, and underneath it, scared.")
4. **My part**: how you co-created or contributed. ("My part is I never told you this deadline actually
   mattered to me.")
5. **Want / request**: the specific, clean request. ("Going forward I'd like us to confirm deadlines
   explicitly. Would you be willing to do that?")

The magic is steps 2 and 4: labeling the story *as a story* and owning your part keeps both people above
the line.

## Shift practices (getting back above the line)

When someone (or you) is below the line, don't problem-solve yet — shift first. Fast moves:

- **Conscious breathing**: a few slow 4-second in/out breaths to settle the nervous system.
- **Change posture / move the body**: literally stand up, shake out; even doing something absurd
  ("hop on one foot while you explain the problem") breaks the grip of seriousness.
- **Move to wonder**: replace a closed statement with an open question — "I wonder what's really going
  on for them?" "I wonder what I'm not seeing?"
- **Choose learning over being right**: ask "would I rather be right here, or would I rather learn?"
- **Locate and feel the emotion** (above) so it can release instead of running the show.
