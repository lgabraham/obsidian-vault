# The Great CEO Within — Matt Mochary

Reference for the tactical operating system. Source: *The Great CEO Within* (Matt Mochary). Organized by
the problem you're trying to solve so you can grab the right tool. Mochary's bias: make things explicit,
write them down, assign one owner, decide fast, and resolve issues directly.

## Personal operating system

**Top Goal.** Each day, spend the first hour (uninterrupted — no email, no Slack, door closed) on the
single most important goal. Most people do reactive work first; the highest-leverage move is to protect
that first hour for the one thing that matters most. One goal, not three.

**Getting Things Done (GTD).** Get every open loop out of your head into a single trusted system. Collect
everything → process each item (if <2 min, do it now; else defer with a date or delegate) → review
regularly. A clear mind comes from trusting the system, not from holding tasks in memory.

**Inbox Zero.** Process email to empty (at least daily). Each email: do, delegate, defer (calendar/task),
or delete/archive. The inbox is a processing queue, not a storage system or a to-do list.

**Energy Audit + Zone of Genius.** List your recurring activities. Mark each as energizing (+) or
draining (−), and as something only you can do vs. something others could do. Then systematically
delegate or eliminate the draining/low-uniqueness work and move toward your **Zone of Genius** — the
work that is both uniquely yours and energizing. (Four zones, after Hendricks: incompetence → competence
→ excellence → genius. Excellence is the trap: you're great at it and it pays, but it drains you.)

**Other personal habits.** *On time and present* (be fully where you are). *When you say it twice, write
it down* — turn any repeated explanation into a wiki doc. *Gratitude and appreciation* as a daily
practice. Protect health, sleep, and recovery as performance inputs, not luxuries.

## Decision-making

- **One decision-maker (DRI — Directly Responsible Individual).** Every decision has a single accountable
  owner. Consensus is slow and diffuses responsibility; assign the decision to the person closest to the
  information.
- **Gather input, then decide.** The DRI solicits input widely (especially from those affected and those
  with expertise), but the decision is theirs — input is not a vote.
- **Disagree and commit.** Once decided, everyone — including dissenters — commits fully. Re-litigating
  after the fact is corrosive.
- **Loudest voice in the room.** The most senior person should speak *last*. If the leader states a view
  first, they anchor the room and suppress real input. Ask others first; reserve your opinion.
- **Reversible vs. irreversible.** Make reversible decisions fast; reserve deliberation for the ones that
  are hard to undo.

## Impeccable agreements

Most organizational dysfunction traces to fuzzy agreements. An impeccable agreement specifies **who will
do what by when**, and all parties give a genuine yes (not a "corporate nod"). If you realize you can't
keep an agreement, **renegotiate it as soon as you know** — proactively, before the deadline — rather
than letting it silently slip. Broken agreements without renegotiation erode trust and should carry
clear, agreed consequences. (This is the tactical twin of Dethmer's Integrity commitment.)

## Issue identification & conflict resolution

Mochary's core move: surface issues early and name them precisely. Distinguish:

- **Transactional issues**: practical, solvable problems ("the deploy keeps breaking," "we missed the
  number"). Handle with the issues process: name the issue → identify the real (often underlying)
  problem → assign a DRI and a by-when.
- **Transformational issues**: interpersonal/emotional friction between people. These don't yield to
  task-assignment; they need a direct, candid conversation (this is exactly where Dethmer's Clearing
  Model and feeling-the-feelings work belong). Trying to "solve" a transformational issue transactionally
  is a common failure mode.

The discipline: don't let issues fester in side-channels or gossip. Get them onto the table (a running
issues list, or directly between the people involved), name them, and resolve them to a clear owner.

## Feedback

- **Ask permission first**: "Are you open to some feedback?" Consent makes it receivable.
- **Praise in public, correct in private.**
- **Use observation, not character**: describe the specific behavior and its impact rather than labeling
  the person ("When the report came in a day late, the client meeting slipped" — not "you're
  unreliable"). Compatible with nonviolent communication and with Dethmer's facts→story→feeling→want.
- **Receiving feedback**: just say "thank you." Don't defend or explain. The goal is to make it safe to
  give you more.
- Build a regular feedback rhythm (e.g., in 1:1s) so it's normal, not an event.

## Meetings (the cadence)

Run a deliberate meeting rhythm rather than ad-hoc calls:

- **Daily** (optional, for tight teams): quick standup / blockers.
- **Weekly tactical**: review metrics/KPIs, surface and assign issues, confirm action items (each with a
  DRI and a date).
- **Monthly or quarterly strategic**: step back to bigger questions, away from the weekly weeds.
- **Quarterly/annual offsite**: planning, goals, relationships.

Every meeting: an agenda, the right people, and action items captured with owners and dates. End by
confirming who-does-what-by-when.

## Organizational infrastructure

- **Areas of Responsibility (AORs)**: every function/area has exactly one accountable owner, documented
  in a single list, so nothing falls through the cracks and there's never ambiguity about who owns what.
- **KPIs**: each AOR has a small number of key metrics, reviewed on the weekly cadence; metrics make
  performance visible and conversations objective.
- **No single point of failure**: cross-train and document so the company doesn't depend on any one
  person's undocumented knowledge.
- **Transparency**: default to open — share financials, board decks, and meeting notes broadly. Trust and
  alignment compound when information is open.
- **The wiki / company folder system**: a single organized source of truth; "when you say it twice, write
  it down" feeds it. Information should be findable, not tribal.

## Conscious Leadership (Mochary's own bridge to Dethmer)

Mochary dedicates a chapter to Conscious Leadership and explicitly draws on the Conscious Leadership
Group. The throughline: a leader's effectiveness is gated by their internal state. Self-awareness,
radical responsibility, and the willingness to feel and release emotion (rather than act them out) are
treated as *operating prerequisites* for the tactics above — which is precisely why this skill pairs the
two. When a Mochary tactic isn't landing, the cause is often an inner-game issue from `dethmer.md`.
