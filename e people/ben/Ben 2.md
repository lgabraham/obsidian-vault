Thank you for sharing. 

I couldn’t help thinking while reading this: greater trust always feels unsafe at first. 

“What it I lose my private key?” Personally, I would trade this “risk” for all my dealings with bankers. I might lose my private key just as I might die in a motorcycle crash.          Zzz

I think this is why the generation divide. I came up through 2008 and a global pandemic. (“Y’all fucked our money.”) I’ll take the risk of managing my private key against the the State’s ability to manage the money supply.

Now generational warfare serves no one. Yet I believe most generations above us are indeed too far out of their paradigm to comprehend this change. (Personally, I am terrified for when this will happen to me!) I observe a complicated pattern of paternalism and mistrust: the State should take care of this, but do they?

I recognize not all are “ready” for such a solution. There are already tools, like Coinbase, that make it more accessible. 

The technical critiques are sort of throw aways here. BTC is “moaty” precisely because it has grown out of its risk of 51% attack. 

Personally, I believe hard money is worth 1% of total energy spend, and the demand being relatively new can easily become renewable. Though I haven’t fully dived into this “problem” and may just be spouting the dogma. 

It’s true BTC’s rules are not perfect. Perhaps too zero-sum, though this feature makes it “hard” and deflationary. I do think the rules are “good enough,” and other block chains can solve other problems. 

The Pornographers and criminals pave the way of technological progress. BTC, though is a fairly transparent ledger, with some folks mapping wallets. It’s the Zero Proof coins that ultimately can’t be tracked. Ultimately, this is a battle for privacy larger than coins. 

Bitcoin is a threat to the dollar. It will spur the US to build a govcoin, like the digital Yuan, which is a panopticon. I do believe all 3 can coexist. I, for one, am glad for BTC’s (or whatever crypto) counterweight.

Yes, the space has charlatans. It is, by nature, a quasi ponzu scheme. Meme-pumping is a problem, but also an effective way to promote adoption of a new generation. Inflation fear mongering is overdone. 

Bitcoin can be stopped. Like Napster was, to use Jason Calacanacis’s analogy on All In. But the decentralized nature of it makes it less likely. 

The Old Men Yelling will die off (Munger, this guy). I don’t mean to be disrespectful. Some of them were heroes of their time. 

As our digitally native generation — a generation who may trust technology more than bureaucracy and who still has today a very low percentage of overall wealth — gains wealth then looks for a place to store it, I’d guess BTC will continue to perform. 

I sent $10K in 40 min. this week without a bank. It was liberating as hell. I sent it from a stock of capital that (I believe) is deflationary (if hyper volatile now). 

I also have a stack of gold coins (also deflationary). To send $10K, I would have to lug them to a dealer, pay 5% to convert to cash, go to a bank (I likely hate) and wire the money for a large fee. 

The difference between the above two worlds is not 10x. It’s 10,000x. 

I took the red pill. I’ve not seen anyone go the other way. I have seen critique from outsiders. 

This one didn’t impress me, though. It makes me want to write a critique of my own, if only to test the hypothesis. There should be debate.
